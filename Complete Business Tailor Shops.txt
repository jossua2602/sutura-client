PHASE 1 — PHILIPPINE REAL-WORLD MARKET RESEARCH
A. Common Business Models in the Philippines (Actual Behavior)

Order Acceptance: Facebook Messenger is the default CRM. Walk-ins are entertained but often take a backseat to bulk FB inquiries. Successful shops use a "Call-to-Action" comment system (e.g., "Comment CUSTOM to get a quote") to bypass Messenger's message request limit.
Bulk Clients: Suki culture is king. Bulk clients (companies, schools, fraternities) are given priority lanes. They usually transact via Viber or WhatsApp groups with the owner directly.
Rush Orders: "Rush" in the Philippines usually means "I need it tomorrow." Real shops charge 30-50% more, OR they push the client to a more expensive DTF/Heat Press method instead of cut-and-sew sublimation to save time. If a client is a suki, the rush fee is often waived but the order is squeezed into overtime.
Downpayments: Strictly 50% DP for custom work to cover fabric and ink. However, for government/corporate accounts, they accept Purchase Orders (PO) and allow 50% DP upon delivery, with 50% balance on terms (15-30 days).
Repeat Customers: They don't use points systems. They use "Free Layout" or "Free 1pc upgrade" for repeat clients. The owner personally chats them when new fabric arrives.
B. Multi-Branch Operational Patterns

The "Bodega" and Satellite Model: The main branch (usually in an industrial/commercial zone like Marikina, Davao, or Cebu) houses the heavy machinery (industrial sublimation printers, 15-needle embroidery, large cutting tables). Satellite branches in malls or busy intersections (e.g., Kalosph's multiple branches) are purely for showroom, measuring, fitting, and pickup.
Logistics: Fabric and printed panels are cut at the main branch, bundled, and sent via Lalamove/Grab Pabili to satellite branches where local mananahi (sewers) assemble them.
Branch Performance: Tracked by total DP collected vs. finished items released. Walk-in retail sales are considered "bonus" revenue; the lifeblood is bulk orders.
C. Philippine Payment Behaviors

Methods: GCash dominates retail/custom. BPI/BDO bank transfers dominate bulk corporate. Maya is secondary. Cash-on-Delivery (COD) is accepted only for trusted sukis or small wholesale RTW.
Practical Flow:
Quotation sent via FB.
Client sends GCash screenshot of 50% DP.
Admin verifies, then triggers layout/mockup creation.
Balance is collected via GCash before shipping, or Cash-on-Pickup at the branch.
D. Seasonal Sales Behavior (Product Spike Mapping)

Apr-May (Summer & Elections): Campaign shirts, barangay uniforms, rash guards, basketball leagues. Sublimation ink and fabric fly off the shelves.

Repair / Warranty Return Flow (Adjustment Orders)
Why: "Sir, maliit yung sukat ng sobrang malaki yung T-shirt." Real shops allow 1-time free adjustment (letting out seams) within 7 days.
Rental Damage Tracking (For Gown/Barong Shops)
Why: Tracks stains, torn lace, or missing buttons. Automatically deducts from the cash bond/refundable deposit.

PHASE 5 — EXPANSION LOGIC (1 → 3 → 5 Branches)
How the business breaks and what becomes mandatory as it scales:

Scaling from 1 to 3 Branches:

The Breakpoint: The owner can no longer be the layout artist, the cutter, and the cashier. Quality control drops.
Mandatory Modules: Centralized Inventory (so Branch B doesn't steal Branch A's fabric), and the Team Builder Matrix (to standardize order intake).
Workflow Fix: Branches must stop doing their own layouts. All layouts centralized at Main. Satellites just take measurements and collect DP.
Bottleneck: Logistics. Moving cut fabric between branches via Grab/Lalamove gets expensive.
Scaling from 3 to 5 Branches (Full Operation):

The Breakpoint: Supplier credit limits and production floor space. 5 branches generating orders will choke a single sublimation printer.
Mandatory Modules:
Automated Procurement: System auto-alerts supplier when sublimation paper drops below 50 rolls.
Employee Shift & Payroll Tracking: Piece-rate (pakiwari) sewers need per-garment payout tracking.
Centralized vs. Local:
Centralized: Sublimation Printing, Embroidery, Pattern Making, Bulk Fabric Storage.
Branch-Local: Sewing (assembly), Heat Press (for small DTF/mugs), Walk-in Fitting, Customer Pickup.
The Ultimate Bottleneck: Machine Downtime. If the 64" industrial sublimation printer breaks down, 5 branches stop earning. The system must track machine maintenance schedules and have a backup outsourced print partner on standby.
Philippine-Specific Practical Recommendations:
Accept "Palengke" Resellers: Create a wholesale tier for market vendors who buy plain sublimated shirts from you to silk-screen themselves. This moves bulk inventory fast.
Integrate Lazada/Shopee/TikTok API: Many PH sublimation shops fulfill orders through marketplace checkouts. The system must pull Shopee orders into the main Job Order queue to avoid double-encoding.
"Pakiwari" (Piece-rate) Payroll Module: Sewers in the PH are usually paid per piece (e.g., ₱50 per jersey sewn). The system must generate a daily/weekly payroll report based on the "Sewing Complete" status updates per sewer.


Here is the raw, operational business intelligence of how real Philippine tailoring, sublimation, and fashion businesses actually run on the ground. This is stripped of all software theory and focused purely on human behavior, physical workflows, and local market realities.

---

### **A. Production Workflow Intelligence**

**1. Inquiry & Quotation Phase**
*   **The Facebook Messenger Reality:** The business does not start in a shop; it starts in Messenger. Customers send screenshots from Pinterest, TikTok, or other pages. The owner manually quotes based on a mental price matrix (fabric type + complexity + quantity).
*   **Walk-in Flow:** Walk-ins are entertained but often put on hold if bulk online orders are pending. The owner or clerk entertains walk-ins, pulls out fabric swatches (or takes them to the "tela" racks), and writes down details in a ledger.
*   **Quotation Behavior:** Quotation is highly fluid. If the client hesitates, the owner offers a downgrade in fabric (e.g., from Microcool to Honeycomb) to lower the price. 

**2. Booking & Measurement Phase**
*   **The "No DP, No Layout" Rule:** Shops do not start layouts or cut fabric without a downpayment. 
*   **Measurements:** 
    *   *Uniforms/Sublimation:* Customers provide a size chart (S-5XL) or send a "sando that fits perfectly" as a reference.
    *   *Tailoring/Gowns/Barong:* Clients must come in person. The *mananahi* (seamstress) takes measurements using a tape measure. Measurements are written on a job order card that physically follows the fabric.
*   **Design Approval:** For sublimation, a layout artist sends a digital mockup via Messenger. The client must explicitly say "Approved" or send a screenshot. For gowns, sketches or peg photos are confirmed before cutting.

**3. Production & Deadline Management**
*   **Batching:** Bulk orders (e.g., 50 basketball jerseys) are never done one by one. Fabric is rolled, cut in stacks using a pattern, then sent to the printer/heat press, then to a row of sewers. 
*   **Deadline Estimation:** Usually quoted as "7-14 working days." Working days are strictly counted *after* DP and layout approval, not from the date of inquiry.
*   **Rush Orders:** If a client demands a 3-day turnaround, the shop charges a 30-50% rush fee. This fee is often used to pay sewers overtime (pakiwari) or to pull the job to the front of the queue, inevitably delaying other clients.

**4. Fittings, Revisions & Release**
*   **Fittings (Gowns/Barong/Suits):** Usually two fittings. First fitting checks the base fit. Second fitting checks the final drape. Revisions are done on the spot (pinning).
*   **Uniforms:** No fittings. Strictly based on the agreed size chart. 
*   **Revisions/Complaints:** If a uniform doesn't fit and it's the shop's fault (wrong cut), they adjust it for free. If the client gave the wrong size, the shop usually charges a fee for re-cutting or offers a discount on a remake.
*   **Release:** "Claiming" is the final step. The shop checks the physical job order ticket, ensures the balance is paid, packs the item, and hands it over. 

**5. Rental Garments vs. Custom Orders**
*   **Rentals (Gowns/Barong/Filipiniana):** The client pays a rental fee (usually 30-50% of the item's retail value) plus a refundable security deposit. The garment is lent out for 2-3 days. The deposit is held to cover damages, stains, or late returns.

---

### **B. Payment Behavior Intelligence**

*   **Standard Downpayment (DP):** 50% is the industry standard. For tailors, this covers fabric and initial labor. For sublimation, it covers ink, transfer paper, and blank garments.
*   **Reservation Fees:** For highly customized gowns or peak-season blocking, a non-refundable ₱1,000 - ₱3,000 reservation fee is collected to "block the slot."
*   **Corporate & Government Billing:** For DTI/BIR registered shops handling schools, LGUs, or corporations, they accept Purchase Orders (PO). Payment terms are usually 50% DP upon signing of PO, and 50% balance upon delivery. Large corporations often ask for 30-day check releases, which forces the tailor to have strong cash flow.
*   **Payment Methods:** GCash dominates retail. Maya is secondary. Bank transfers (BDO/BPI) are used for corporate. Walk-ins still heavily use cash.
*   **Verification Process:** Admins manually check GCash screenshots. Fake screenshots are a common scam. Many require the client to send the GCash reference number first before packing.
*   **Full Payment Before Release:** Strictly enforced. "No balance, no claim." Even if the client is a *suki*, the item stays in the shop until the final peso is paid, especially before holidays or weekends.
*   **Refunds & Cancellations:** If the client cancels after DP is paid, the DP is forfeited to cover the cost of fabric already cut and ink already printed. Refunds are rarely given for custom items; only for gross shop negligence.

---

### **C. Customer Retention Intelligence**

*   **The "Suki" System:** Loyalty is not tracked by points; it’s tracked by memory and relationship. A *suki* gets priority slots, waived rush fees, or free upgrades (e.g., using a better zipper for free).
*   **Saved Measurements:** Local tailors keep a physical "Suki Card" or an Excel sheet of repeat clients' measurements. When a client messages, "Pwede ba akong palitan ng polo same size as last year?", the tailor just pulls the old record. No need to retake measurements.
*   **Family-Based Clients:** A shop that makes a wedding gown often ends up making the debut gown for the daughter 18 years later, and the barongs for the sons. Keeping family records is crucial.
*   **Corporate Uniform Retention:** B2B clients are retained through consistency. If a company orders 200 polos this year, the tailor keeps the pattern and logo files so that next year, when new hires come in, they can just say "Add 10 pcs to the ABC Corp order."
*   **Design History:** Keeping the client’s approved layout files in the archive. If they want to reprint the exact same jersey next season, the shop doesn't charge a layout fee anymore.

---

### **D. Seasonal Demand Intelligence**

*   **March - April (Graduation & Summer):** Massive spike in graduation sashes, toga alterations, PE uniforms, and summer league basketball jerseys (barangay intrams). This is a "no sleep" season.
*   **May (Elections & Fiestas):** Every 3 years, election season is a goldmine. Campaign shirts (DTF or Silk Screen) are ordered by the thousands. Fiesta seasons also demand uniformed organizations (Knights of Columbus, Coca-Cola bearers).
*   **June - July (Back to School):** School uniforms, patches, and ID lanyards.
*   **August - October (Company Teambuildings & Sports Fest):** Corporate jerseys, barkada shirts, and company hoodies. 
*   **November - December (Ber Months/Weddings/Debuts):** Corporate Christmas party shirts, customized mugs/tumblers for giveaways, and the peak season for bridal gowns, Barongs, and Filipiniana for December weddings.
*   **Dead Months:** January to mid-February. Shops survive this by offering RTW (Ready-to-Wear) customization, accepting repair jobs (rasing/adjusting old pants), or pushing Valentine's couple shirts.

---

### **E. Scaling & Multi-Branch Intelligence**

*   **The Main-Satellite Model:** Successful shops (e.g., in Davao or Manila) do not put heavy machinery in malls or expensive retail spaces. The Main Branch (the *bodega*) is in a cheaper industrial area. It houses the industrial sublimation printers, 15-needle embroidery machines, and cutting tables. Satellite branches in malls or busy intersections are purely for taking measurements, accepting walk-ins, and pickups.
*   **Production Routing:** Cut fabric from the Main branch is sent via local courier (Lalamove/Grab) to home-based *mananahis* (sewers) or to satellite branches for assembly. 
*   **Branch Specialization:** Some branches become known for specific things. Branch A handles all sublimation. Branch B handles all corporate tailoring. This prevents machine bottlenecks.
*   **Oversight:** The owner usually stays at the Main branch to oversee production quality. Satellite branches are run by trusted "encoders" or head seamstresses who report daily sales via Viber.
*   **Expansion Triggers:** Shops open a new branch when the main shop's capacity is maxed out, and clients from a specific city (e.g., clients from Taytay traveling to Puerto Princesa) complain about the distance.

### **FINAL OUTPUT: BUSINESS LOGIC PATTERNS & PAIN POINTS**

**Most Common Operational Workflows:**
1.  **The Messenger Bounce:** Inquiry -> Verbal Quote -> 50% DP sent via GCash -> Mockup approved -> Fabric cut -> Print/Sew -> Photo sent to client -> Balance sent -> Pickup.
2.  **The Corporate PO Flow:** RFQ (Request for Quotation) -> Send Proposal -> Sign Contract/PO -> 50% DP -> Bulk Production -> Delivery -> 30-day wait for remaining 50% balance.

**Weak Points & Bottlenecks:**
*   **The Layout Bottleneck:** Layout artists take too long, delaying printing. 
*   **Fabric Shortages:** Running out of a specific color mid-bulk-order because the supplier in Divisoria/Bambang ran out.
*   **Sewing Bottlenecks:** Sublimation printing is fast; sewing is slow. A shop can print 100 jerseys in a day but only sew 30. 

**What Causes Delays & Customer Complaints:**
*   **Delays:** Late layout approvals, late DP payments, machine breakdowns (heat press element burning out), and sewer absence (mananahi not showing up).
*   **Complaints:** Shrinking after washing (using wrong fabric/print temp), wrong sizing (client gave wrong measurements), color fading (using DTF on dark shirts improperly), and missing deadlines for events (e.g., jersey wasn't ready for the championship game).

**What Makes Customers Return:**
*   Trust. The fabric didn't peel. The print didn't fade. The uniform fit perfectly. The owner was easy to talk to and allowed a minor revision without throwing a fit. 

**What Makes Businesses Scale vs. Fail:**
*   **Scale:** Delegation. The owner stops doing layouts and cutting fabric. They hire a dedicated graphic artist, a master cutter, and encoders. They centralize procurement to get bulk fabric discounts.
*   **Fail:** Accepting too many rush orders during peak season, leading to a catastrophic drop in quality. Ruining a 100-piece corporate order means losing a B2B client forever. Another major cause of failure is poor cash flow management—spending the 50% DP of a future order to pay off past expenses, leaving no money to buy fabric for the current order.

**Modern Social Selling Patterns:**
*   "Comment a keyword to claim a slot" to bypass Messenger limits.
*   Posting "Order Released Today" photos on Facebook as a public trust-building mechanism (showing the shop actually delivers).
*   Selling lifetime-access Google Drive folders of 30,000+ designs for ₱399—a passive income stream that requires zero physical labor.




### 1. RENTAL LIFECYCLE MODULE (Kulang sa Fashion Shop Research)
Sa research mo, malinaw na ang mga fashion shops ay kumikita sa "Hybrid Rental + MTO". Kasama dito ang strict rules sa pag-rent.
*   **Dapat Idagdag:** 
    *   **Valid ID Tracking:** Kailangan may field sa system na nagta-track ng "1 Valid ID Captured" bago ma-release ang rental item. (Ito yung pambabrazen sa mga magnanakaw ng gown).
    *   **Hygiene/Cleaning Status:** Ang isang gown ay pwede ma-rent ngayon, pero kapag naibalik, hindi agad pwede ma-rent bukas kasi "Marumi/Pang-press pa". Kailangan may toggle sa Catalog item na `Needs Cleaning` vs `Ready to Rent`.
    *   **Damage Tracking & Deposit Deduction:** Kapag naibalik na may mantsa ang gown, kailangan may field sa "Return Inspection" para i-log kung magkano ang bawas sa Refundable Deposit dahil sa damage.

### 2. B2B / CORPORATE ACCOUNT FIELDS (Kulang sa Sublimation Shop Research)
Ang research mo ay puno ng "Bulk Clients, Corporate Accounts, LGU, at Schools". Ibang klase ang flow ng bayaran nila.
*   **Dapat Idagdag:**
    *   **Purchase Order (PO) Number:** Ang corporate clients ay nagpapadala ng PO, hindi lang simpleng order. Kailangan may PO Number field sa Job Order.
    *   **Payment Terms Dropdown:** Hindi lahat ay 50% DP agad. Kailangan may options sa Job Order para sa "50% DP, 50% upon delivery" o "30-day Net Terms" para sa mga government/corporate.

### 3. PHYSICAL JOB TICKET GENERATOR (Kulang sa Production Workflow)
Sinabi sa research mo: *"Measurements are written on a job order card that physically follows the fabric."* at *"Must support a QR code or Barcode printed on a physical tag."*
*   **Dapat Idagdag:**
    *   **Printable Job Ticket (PDF):** Ang system mo ay dapat may "Print Job Ticket" button na maglalabas ng isang print-ready card na may QR code. Dito nakalagay ang Order #, Customer Name, Fabric Type, at Measurements. Ipi-print ito ng staff at itatahi sa tela habang gumagawa sila.

### 4. FABRIC SWATCH SHOWCASE (Kulang sa Catalog)
Sinabi sa research: *"Fabric Swatch Catalog: Clients need to feel the fabric... Honeycomb, Polydex, Microcool, Aircool with actual GSM."* 
Kahit bawal ang Inventory sa thesis scope mo, hindi bawal ang "Showcase".
*   **Dapat Idagdag:** 
    *   Isang tab sa Design Catalog o Shop Settings na "Fabric Swatches". Dito mag-uupload ang shop owner ng larawan ng tela (Mesh, Cocoon, Piña) na may label ng presyo at texture notes. Para makita ng customer kung anong klaseng tela ang gagamitin.

### 5. RUSH ORDER LOGIC & ALERTS (Kulang sa Operations)
Sinabi sa research: *"Rush in the Philippines usually means I need it tomorrow. Real shops charge 30-50% more."*
*   **Dapat Idagdag:**
    *   Sa paggawa ng Custom Job, kailangan may Checkbox na **"Rush Order?"**. 
    *   Kapag na-click ito, lalabas ang field para sa **"Rush Fee (₱)"** at aangat ang priority ng order sa Dashboard (magkakaroon ng red flag o ⚡ icon). 
    *   Dapat rin itong mag-aadjust ng Estimated Pickup Date (e.g., instead ng 14 days, 3 days na lang).

### 6. PEAK SEASON CAPACITY BLOCKER (Kulang sa Appointments)
Sinabi sa research: *"During peak season, production reaches capacity. Shops need a Cutoff date."*
*   **Dapat Idagdag:**
    *   Sa Appointments o Custom Jobs, kailangan may "Capacity Limit" per araw. Halimbawa, kung 50 jerseys na ang booked para sa October 15, dapat magiging "Fully Booked" na ang araw na iyon at hindi na makaka-accept ng bagong delivery date sa October 15. Aabante na lang siya sa October 20.

### 7. SUKI TAGGING SYSTEM (Kulang sa CRM)
Sinabi sa research: *"Loyalty is not tracked by points; it’s tracked by memory... A suki gets priority slots, waived rush fees."*
*   **Dapat Idagdag:**
    *   Sa Customer Profile, kailangan may "Suki Level" o "Tags" (e.g., B2B Suki, Walk-in Retail, Reseller). 
    *   Kapag B2B Suki ang nag-order, pwede siya magkaroon ng "Waived Rush Fee" o "Free Layout" na auto-apply sa kanyang Job Order base sa kanyang tag.

### 8. "NO DP, NO LAYOUT" STRICT ENFORCEMENT (Kulang sa Business Logic)
Sinabi sa research: *"Shops do not start layouts or cut fabric without a downpayment."*
*   **Dapat Idagdag:**
    *   Sa UI ng Custom Jobs Kanban, dapat naka-lock (grayed out) ang "Layout Design" at "Cutting" stages kapag walang naka-log na Downpayment sa Payment Center. Hindi siya pwede i-drag and drop sa kabilang stage hangga't walang DP.

Introduction
Project Context
Digital visibility is critical for traditional tailoring businesses. Local consumers often struggle to find specialized tailors due to a lack of centralized business profiles and fragmented market information. Centralized tracking systems featuring localized mapping and clear apparel categorization help bridge this gap, making digital integration essential for modern tailoring practices.
Globally, the bespoke garment industry is undergoing a digital transformation as consumers shift away from mass-produced fast fashion toward personalized apparel (Statista, 2025). This evolving craft has become a highly competitive sector of commerce. Consequently, tailoring enterprises worldwide are rapidly adopting web-based storefronts and software-as-a-service (SaaS) frameworks to secure a digital presence in key urban hubs.
In the Philippines, this digital shift is reflected in national directives urging micro, small, and medium enterprises (MSMEs) to adopt online tools to match modern consumer behavior (DTI, 2024). Although the demand for uniforms, formal wear, and corporate attire is consistently high, the local garment industry remains fragmented. Philippine tailoring shops lack centralized digital ecosystems to display their work, creating a disconnect between tech-savvy Filipino consumers and traditional shop owners.
Despite the growing demand for custom garments in Davao City, consumers continue to face significant challenges in locating the right tailoring shop for their specific needs. There is currently no centralized digital platform where customers can search for tailoring services by garment type such as Filipiniana, Barong Tagalog, or school uniforms and immediately see all available shops within their area. This forces customers to rely on word-of-mouth recommendations or physically travel across different districts of the city just to find a tailor that specializes in their required garment. This fragmented discovery process leads to wasted time, missed revenue opportunities for local tailors, and reduced customer satisfaction.
These manual setups cause critical operational challenges, leading to unnecessary travel and miscommunication for customers trying to verify shop capabilities. This visibility gap results in prolonged search times, missed revenue opportunities for local tailors, and reduced customer satisfaction during peak seasons. Fragmented data also makes it difficult for shop owners and system administrators to evaluate marketplace trends, generate performance reports, or monitor active subscriptions.
Studies confirm that centralized discovery and tracking platforms are vital for service-driven industries. Localized mapping systems significantly improve consumer engagement by providing immediate access to branch locations and operating hours (Mapme, 2024). Additionally, automated notifications and transparent tracking features build consumer trust and optimize retention when embedded into service workflows (Zendesk, 2024).
To resolve these challenges, this project proposes SUTURA: A Web-Based Tailoring Shop Tracker System. The system integrates subscription-based tier management, localized branch mapping, specialized apparel categorization, and dynamic order progress tracking. This system is designed to support both consumer-facing search inquiries and structured tailoring shop workflows, increasing digital market visibility while providing a data-driven discovery experience.









Purpose and Description
Many traditional tailoring shops still rely on manual, fragmented methods to maintain business visibility and handle consumer inquiries. Service specializations are rarely cataloged online and location details are shared inconsistently through phone calls or word-of-mouth, often resulting in prolonged search times and double appointmet during peak seasons. While these offline approaches may suffice for small-scale local markets, they ultimately lead to missed revenue opportunities for tailors and reduced discovery satisfaction for customers. 

To address these issues, this project proposes the development of SUTURA: A Web-Based Tailoring Shop Tracker System. The system is designed to provide system administrators, shop owners, and customers with a centralized network that simplifies and organizes shop discoverability and service tracking. Instead of relying on disconnected offline methods, SUTURA integrates localized branch mapping, specialized apparel categorization, subscription tier management, and dynamic order tracking into a single system. 

SUTURA operates through a tier-based subscription offering Basic, Pro, and Premium plans that allows tailoring shop owners to register their business and manage multiple branches based on their selected tier. The system supports storefront customization, branch location mapping, apparel specialization management, and automated order status tracking through a unified tailoring shop dashboard. By restricting public visibility and premium features to active subscribers, the system ensures a verified, organized, and reliable marketplace for consumers. 

For daily tracking, SUTURA provides a centralized Tailoring Shop Management Dashboard that enables tailoring shop owners to manage active customer inquiries, update shop branches, and monitor order progress in real time. Shop staff can update the stages of garment fulfillment such as cutting, sewing, and ready for pickup while recording basic consumer transaction details for subscription performance metrics. All updates are instantly synchronized across the system, ensuring that customers receive real-time visibility regarding their order status and verified shop availability. 
The system also includes a web-based public discovery platform tailored for customers. Users can search for local tailoring shops, filter businesses by specific apparel specializations, view real-time branch locations on an interactive map, and read verified customer reviews. Additionally, customers can monitor their ongoing garment fulfillment status in real time and receive automated notifications, ensuring a transparent, convenient, and reliable transaction process from discovery to pickup. 

To ensure platform quality and reliability, SUTURA implements a strict pre-verification process for all newly registered businesses. Upon creating an account, shop owners must first undergo a thorough review and approval process by the system administrator before gaining any access to the system or its internal dashboard features. This approach guarantees that only fully verified and legitimate tailoring establishments can configure shop settings, access internal management workflows, and ultimately appear on the public marketplace and interactive map interface. 















Objectives
To centralize and digitize the discoverability and service tracking of tailoring shops within Davao City by providing integrated tools for location-based shop discovery, tier-based subscription management, real-time order progress monitoring, and data-driven business analytics, ultimately improving both the tailoring shop's digital visibility and the customer's service experience
Specifically, this study aims to:
	•	Administrative Dashboard for subscription monitoring, the system administrator will manage tailoring shop registration approvals, validate apparel categories, verify branch map locations, and oversee platform-wide activity and system performance. 
	•	Subscription and Account Management for tiered plan enrollment, the tailoring shop will register on the system, select from Basic, Pro, and Premium subscription plans, manage role-based staff access, and maintain active subscription validity for continued platform visibility. 
	•	Shop Discovery and Map-Based Navigation Module for location and garment-based search, the customer will search for tailoring services by specific garment type, filter results by area, specialization, and real-time branch availability, view pinned geolocation coordinates of verified tailoring shops within Davao City, and access complete storefront profiles and route directions. 
	•	Tailoring Shop Dashboard for storefront management, the shop owner will configure their digital profile, manage service catalogs and apparel specializations, set itemized pricing, handle appointment schedules, and control their public platform visibility. 
	•	Order Tracking and Measurement Module for real-time production monitoring, the tailoring staff will record and retrieve digital customer measurements, manage appointment-based order intake, and update garment production stages, while customers monitor their order progress from placement to pickup.
	•	Interactive Analytics Dashboard for data-driven reporting, the system administrator and tailoring shop owners will generate real-time visual reports on subscription activity, monthly sales revenue, outstanding customer balances, and individual shop productivity. 




























Scope and Limitations
Scope of the Study
The scope of this study covers the development and implementation of SUTURA: A Web-Based Tailoring Shop Tracker System, designed to streamline shop discoverability and service visibility through an integrated platform within Davao City's local garment sector. The system supports four primary user roles, namely the system administrator, tailoring shop owners, tailoring staff, and customers, by utilizing a centralized subscription-based network that integrates tier-based shop management, localized branch mapping, specialized apparel categorization, and dynamic order progress tracking. 

The system includes an Administrative Dashboard that enables system administrators to review and approve tailoring shop verification requests before system access is granted, oversee active shop profiles, and validate custom apparel categories. It also monitors tier-based subscription billing, verifies submitted branch map locations, and generates real-time platform performance reports.  	
The Subscription and Account Management enables tailoring shop owners to register their businesses and select from tiered service plans Basic, Pro, and Premium based on their operational needs, subject to administrator pre-verification. Each subscription enforces role-based access boundaries, allowing active tailoring shop owners to curate shop profiles, manage service catalogs, and maintain digital storefronts. 

For customers, the system provides a Shop Discovery and Map-Based Navigation Module that integrates pinned geolocation coordinates to display administrator-approved tailoring shop branches within Davao City. This module features garment-based and location-based filtering, allowing users to search by specific garment type, filter by area or specialization, and view real-time branch availability. Through this unified interface, clients can access complete shop profiles, operational schedules, itemized pricing, and route directions to selected tailoring shop branches. 

The system provides a Tailoring Shop Dashboard built for storefront management, allowing them to configure their digital profiles, manage service catalogs, and declare apparel specializations. This module supports setting itemized pricing configurations, handling customer appointment schedules, and controlling public platform visibility to effectively manage their digital presence on the system. 

The module also supports appointment-based order intake where tailoring staff can create job orders directly from scheduled fittings. Customers can monitor the real-time production status of their garments and receive automated SMS or email notifications at each stage transition, from placement to final pickup.

The Interactive Analytics Dashboard aggregates platform-wide and shop-level data to generate real-time visual reports for both system administrators and shop owners. Administrators can monitor subscription activity, total registered shops, and platform revenue trends, while shop owners can track monthly sales performance, outstanding customer balances, individual staff productivity, and order completion rates. 












Limitations of the Study
This study is limited to software-based operational functionalities and does not include direct integration with physical hardware systems, manufacturing machinery, or automated textile equipment.
The system requires a stable internet connection for all functionalities including real-time order tracking, measurement record access, appointment scheduling, subscription processing, notification delivery, and analytics generation. SUTURA does not support offline data processing or background synchronization, meaning all dashboard operations and real-time updates are unavailable without an active network connection. 
The system does not support hardware integration such as automated body-scanning devices, RFID tags, or digital measuring tools. All customer clothing measurements must be manually encoded by authorized users into the digital repository.
The payment module tracks transaction details and deposits but does not include native integration with third-party payment gateways for direct fund transfers. External financial reconciliation and banking settlement verification are outside the scope of the system.
The reporting module is limited to operational and performance-based summaries and does not include predictive analytics, AI-driven forecasting, or advanced business intelligence. Furthermore, notification delivery depends on third-party services which may result in occasional delays.
The tracking of utility costs such as electricity bills and equipment maintenance is not included as the system focuses primarily on service production and order fulfillment rather than full-scale overhead accounting.
The management of employee payroll including salary calculations and attendance-based wage distribution is outside the functional scope of the system .
The system focuses strictly on service tracking and order fulfillment, completely excluding inventory tracking, material stock monitoring, and purchase order management. Consequently, all supplier-related processes, fabric procurement, and material stock logistics fall entirely outside the system boundary and must be handled independently by the tailoring shop through external means.
The system does not include tax filing automation or business permit validation. It is restricted to managing the internal workflows of tailoring businesses operating within the SUTURA ecosystem.
The system completely excludes logistics, courier routing, and physical delivery management. All garment handovers, order pickups, and shipping arrangements fall outside the system boundary and must be handled directly between the tailoring shop and the customer through external methods.












REVIEW OF RELATED LITERATURE/STUDIES
Tailoring management challenges are well documented in research. Ospina-Agudelo (2024) conducted a mixed-methods study on bespoke tailoring consumer expectations. The study gathered digital behavior analytics from hundreds of active customers. Findings revealed that manual tracking causes significant market visibility loss. Fragmented apparel categorization and absent storefront profiles compound these visibility issues. The author argued that tailors need purpose-built platforms for shop discovery and service tracking. This data-driven research relied on multi-criteria decision-making models to analyze practicing customers. However, the study remained purely exploratory. It did not propose or test any specific digital software solution. This leaves a critical gap that SUTURA is designed to address.
Building on this, Wu et al. (2022) examined manual operational bottlenecks within localized garment networks. Their study found that fragmented, paper-based logs severely diminish the overall customer experience. Outdated methods cause systemic miscommunications regarding customer profiles and fabric types. Furthermore, manual shops face high administrative friction during peak production seasons. This research focuses deeply on internal workspace flaws and advocates for digitized governance models. Meanwhile, Ospina-Agudelo (2024) provides a complementary view regarding external consumer visibility demands. Together, both sources demonstrate that problems exist simultaneously on the customer and tailoring shop sides. Customers expect digital accessibility that manual storefront infrastructure simply cannot provide. Neither source proposes a unified system solving marketplace discovery and internal shop tracking concurrently. This combined problem is exactly what SUTURA is built to resolve.
Workflow and progress tracking represents another critical area validating the need for integrated web solutions. Lapuz (2022) studied the effects of migrating physical logs to a web-based Database Management System. This system utilized a three-tier architecture and the Rapid Application Development methodology. Transitioning to this digital framework drastically accelerated internal business data velocity. The system effectively minimized operational data loss for small-scale retail operators. Furthermore, interactive analytics dashboards allowed business owners to monitor real-time sales and productivity. This provided strong evidence that centralized tracking technology successfully eliminates enterprise inefficiencies. However, the tested system was a general administrative retail tool. It completely lacked the specific sub-modules required for customized garment businesses. It could not store complex body dimensions or track tailoring workflows. SUTURA addresses this limitation by integrating a specialized order and measurement repository.
User acceptance and system usability are fundamental to implementing digital trackers in local communities. Berris (2022) analyzed a Web-Based Membership Tracking Information System using open-source technologies. This research employed the Scrum framework and the ADDIE model to evaluate user experiences. Evaluation was completed through the standardized System Usability Scale. The study found that non-technical staff achieved exceptionally high satisfaction scores. Usability increased dramatically when complex digital databases replaced tedious pen-and-paper logging. Customized applications held a measurable operational advantage over manual environments by preventing inconsistencies. However, this local research was strictly restricted to a martial arts member lifecycle. It did not account for localized branch mapping or specialized apparel workflows. This remains a gap because tailoring operations rely heavily on geographical discoverability. SUTURA addresses this by combining intuitive open-source architecture with localized branch map verification. This configuration ensures high usability while fully catering to the local garment sector.






Review of Related Systems (RRS)
Virtual Cloth Fitting Try-On Network (VCFN) allows users to virtually test garment fits. Its architecture relies heavily on deep learning models and UNet generator layers. The system maps clothing deformation using complex thin-plate spline transformations. VCFN mathematically aligns physical clothing metrics to specific human body vectors. This extreme simulation accuracy effectively minimizes the necessity for physical fittings. However, the system requires immense computing power and specialized graphics hardware. This creates an immediate operational barrier for traditional, low-resource shops. Furthermore, the software focuses strictly on AI graphics simulations. It completely lacks functional back-office business administration modules. SUTURA resolves this gap by providing a lightweight, cloud-based tracking workflow.
Tailor Brands Platform coordinates small business identities and branded corporate merchandise. Its structure features an automated graphic engine linked to a cloud dashboard. This platform provides highly responsive interfaces optimized for non-technical users. This simplified layout sets an excellent usability benchmark for traditional artisans. However, the software focuses primarily on graphic branding and asset continuity. It lacks granular system control for complex, custom tailoring workflows. The system does not host dedicated customer body dimension repositories. It provides no mechanism to monitor real-time production within workshops. SUTURA addresses this by shifting focus to physical artisan tracking mechanics.
The Bespoke Tailor Hub Tracker replaces manual ledger books for boutique retail spaces. Its architecture reflects a standard web-based three-tier design pattern, processing custom order reservation data through real-time operational status filters. The system automatically accumulates monthly commercial sales data and fabric processing updates for internal shop management, effectively balancing rapid system response times with strict data security protocols. However, the application remains restricted to basic, high-level order logging functions. It is entirely incapable of handling custom, item-level apparel tracking, and the underlying database cannot log complex customer anatomical measurement fields. It offers no technical method to track individual garments across physical workshop production phases, such as cutting and sewing. SUTURA directly addresses this gap by adapting this status-tracking logic specifically for sequential, multi-stage bespoke garment production.
The Open-Source Tailoring Shop Tracker automates structural profile tracking for local dressmaking associations. This software relies on standard relational database management structures and achieved high marks on the standardized System Usability Scale, proving that intuitive, minimalist web configurations can easily accommodate non-technical tailoring staff. Furthermore, the system's role-based security module securely preserves customer dimension data profiles over long periods. However, its functional scope is restricted to non-commercial, localized organization tracking. It completely excludes transactional accounting logs, fabric stock depletion alerts, and multi-tier supplier ordering engines. The system also lacks any map-based business discoverability or online scheduling tools for external consumers. SUTURA solves these combined limitations by pairing a highly usable, accessible interface with specialized, end-to-end commercial tracking features.

Synthesis
The four reviewed systems demonstrate that functional tailoring management software exists across a range of operational areas including basic order logging, customer profile preservation, user interface configuration, and virtual fitting. The Virtual Cloth Fitting Try-On Network (VCFN) provides a strong body-vector simulation with deep-learning configurations but completely lacks back-office business administration, customer dimension records, and internal tracking modules. The Tailor Brands Platform offers highly responsive interfaces suited for small brand setup but restricts itself strictly to graphic continuity and corporate assets, entirely lacking physical workshop tracking and customer measurement matrices. The Bespoke Tailor Hub Tracker provides solid high-level order logging and data security protocols but cannot process item-level garment changes, log complex customer anatomical measurement fields, or monitor sequential phase updates. The Open-Source Tailoring Shop Tracker delivers basic profile tracking optimized for non-technical staff but excludes transactional accounting logs and online appointment scheduling.
None of the four systems provides a unified technical workflow that brings together client-accessible appointment scheduling, precise anatomical measurement logging, and multi-stage garment production tracking in a single platform. None of them also provides a map-based interface that allows clients to visually locate registered tailoring shop branches near them, which is a practical feature for first-time clients who are looking for local artisan workshops. These consistent gaps across all reviewed systems establish the functional need for SUTURA, which is designed to integrate all of these workflows into one platform with role-appropriate interfaces for administrators, tailoring shop owners, tailoring staff, and customers.

METHODOLOGY
The development of SUTURA adopts a Developmental Research Approach using the Agile Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC). This approach is highly suitable for the proposed system because it manages a complex architectural framework divided into multiple specialized modules, such as Shop Discovery, Order Tracking, and Interactive Analytics. Since SUTURA is designed to bridge the functional gaps in existing systems by unifying map-based shop visibility and secure measurement versioning, the Agile methodology provides the necessary flexibility to refine these unique features through iterative cycles.
The development process is broken down into specific sprint cycles, with each cycle producing a working version of the system’s features. Rather than building everything at once, each module is designed, tested, and polished before moving to the next. This iterative approach allows for constant feedback and improvements, making it easier to adjust to new requirements. The Agile sprint cycle followed in the development process is illustrated in Figure 3.
The project starts by gathering data through interviews and observations with tailoring shop owners, tailoring staff, and customers. The researchers examine current manual tasks like plotting physical order receipts, documenting customer dimensions, and managing manual appointment schedules to identify common problems and inefficiencies. This information establishes the foundation for the system's technical and functional requirements, ensuring the software directly addresses the specific needs of the local tailoring business.
The project moves into planning and design by organizing the gathered features into a prioritized backlog based on their importance and technical needs. The system is then modeled using use case diagrams, BPMN workflows, activity diagrams, sequence diagrams, database design, and user interface prototypes. These technical blueprints serve as a detailed guide for development, ensuring every part of the system stays aligned with the project goals.
During the actual development phase, the system is built through consecutive sprints, starting with the setup of secure login credentials and role-based access for system administrators, tailoring shop owners, and workshop staff. Subsequent cycles focus on building the core subscription tier tools alongside the localized garment discovery and search module to handle digital shop filtering by location. Later sprints introduce the interactive map-based interface for branch visibility within Davao City, the customer appointment scheduling system, and the automated notification modules. The specialized order tracking and measurement module is then constructed to let staff log unique body profiles and update physical production stages, while giving clients real-time visibility from placement to pickup. Finally, the centralized interactive analytics dashboard is integrated to provide real-time reports on sales revenue, outstanding customer balances, and platform activity.
To maintain high quality, the researchers perform continuous testing during every sprint. Each feature is tested individually and as part of the whole system to ensure everything works correctly. Selected tailoring shop owners, staff, and clients then participate in User Acceptance Testing (UAT) to check if the system is easy to use and helpful for their daily tasks. The feedback from these tests is used to fix issues and improve the software in the next sprint, making the final platform reliable and practical.
Once the sprints are complete, the system is launched in a cloud environment for easy access. After deployment, the researchers continue to monitor the system to fix bugs, improve speed, and ensure data security. Regular maintenance and future updates are also scheduled based on user feedback to keep the system efficient and up to date for tailoring shops and their customers.















Requirement Gathering 
The requirements gathering for SUTURA: A Web-Based Tailoring Shop Tracker System involved a comprehensive data collection process utilizing semi-structured interviews, direct on-site observations, and market-gap analyses within the local tailoring industry of Davao City. The researchers engaged with both tailoring shop owners (representing potential platform subscribers) and local consumers to map out the current fragmented discovery process and identify critical operational bottlenecks. 
The preliminary assessment of the local tailoring sector in Davao City revealed that current operations rely almost entirely on fragmented, manual processes and isolated physical documentation. Customer exploration and shop discovery happen strictly through word-of-mouth recommendations or physical foot traffic, leaving traditional tailors without any unified digital storefront visibility. This disconnected setup creates severe operational and market challenges for both shop owners and consumers. 
The traditional tailoring shop's management acts as the centralized hub for all administrative and operational tasks, from taking initial physical measurements to manually trying to maintain local market visibility. Relying on a single person or a small team to handle consumer inquiries and order fulfillment especially during high-demand peak seasons for school uniforms, formal wear, and corporate attire creates massive operational strain. This heavy manual workload and reliance on fragmented data often lead to severe miscommunication between shop owners and consumers trying to verify shop capabilities or track active orders. 
The assignment of production tasks and overall order fulfillment workflows within the local tailoring industry are currently managed through informal, experience-based methods. Order tracking typically relies on manual record-keeping and memory, meaning progress monitoring is entirely dependent on immediate staff awareness rather than a structured digital workflow tracker. Without a centralized digital ecosystem to monitor garments across sequential production stages, shop owners face significant operational friction. This lack of transparent tracking features makes it exceptionally difficult to communicate progress to consumers or manage high volumes of custom apparel requests such as Filipiniana, Barong Tagalog, and school uniforms especially during peak seasons. 
Difficulties in managing production timelines and tracking active orders were identified as a critical, recurring issue within the local tailoring industry. Because operational data is currently managed across scattered, manual setups and physical documentation, tracking errors frequently occur. This makes it highly challenging for shop owners to handle multiple high-priority orders simultaneously while keeping their actual shop capabilities up to date. This fragmented data environment prevents system administrators and shop owners from effectively evaluating marketplace trends or generating accurate performance reports. 
Significant consumer-facing limitations were identified within the current ecosystem, primarily driven by a lack of digital visibility and fragmented market information. Under the existing manual setups, local consumers completely lack access to automated notifications or transparent, real-time status updates regarding their garment’s progress. Furthermore, tech-savvy consumers have no centralized platform to execute specialized apparel categorization or visually locate nearby services. This forces clients to rely entirely on word-of-mouth recommendations or undergo the inconvenient process of physically traveling across different districts of Davao City just to verify a shop's capabilities. 
To address these operational gaps and market fragmentation, SUTURA: A Web-Based Tailoring Shop Tracker System is designed as a centralized platform that streamlines tailoring workflows while providing a data-driven discovery experience for consumers. To bridge the disconnect between tech-savvy Filipino consumers and traditional shop owners, the system integrates a specialized apparel categorization module, allowing customers in Davao City to search for specialized custom garments such as Filipiniana, Barong Tagalog, or school uniforms and filter results by specific garment types. It embeds a localized branch mapping interface to provide immediate visual access to branch locations and operating hours across different districts, successfully eliminating unnecessary travel and prolonged consumer search times. For structured shop workflows, SUTURA features a dynamic order progress tracking module coupled with automated customer notifications to build trust, optimize retention, and eliminate miscommunication during peak seasonal surges.


Requirements Analysis 
Within the Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC), the requirements analysis phase is essential for pinpointing and establishing the precise needs of both the users and the software. For the proposed system, this stage concentrates on eliminating the bottlenecks associated with traditional, manual tailoring workflows by designing a unified, automated digital tracking and discovery platform.
The system involves four primary user roles: system administrators, tailoring shop owners, tailoring staff, and customers. System administrators manage shop registration approvals, validate custom apparel categories, verify branch map locations, and monitor platform-wide activity and subscription compliance. Meanwhile, tailoring shop owners and staff configure digital profiles, set itemized service pricing, coordinate appointment schedules, version customer measurements, and track production workflows. Finally, customers browse verified storefront profiles, execute garment-based filtering, schedule service-related bookings, and track their garment production progress in real-time. 
The system operates within a web-based environment accessible through standard web browsers. Shop owners and tailoring staff primarily access the system via desktop interfaces for comprehensive workshop management and structural data entry, while the system features a mobile-responsive design for customers. This allows clients to search for tailoring services, utilize the map-based interface, and monitor their order progress remotely from their mobile devices. The system is specifically designed for use within the physical premises of registered tailoring shops in Davao City, as well as remotely by their respective clients.
The system must support key functional requirements, including a map-driven shop discovery matrix and an automated production tracking module. Timing and triggers are central to the system’s operation: automated notifications are triggered instantly when a client books an appointment or when a garment transitions to a new production stage, such as cutting, stitching, fitting, or ready for pickup. Furthermore, business reports and analytics are generated dynamically, providing system administrators with metrics on subscription activity and shop owners with immediate, real-time insights into monthly sales revenue, outstanding customer balances, and individual staff productivity. The system supports flexible billing through deposit tracking and cash or digital gateways, integrated with a SaaS-based subscription tier module Basic, Pro, and Premium for efficient visibility management.
Non-functional requirements include reliability, security, usability, and scalability. The system shall maintain high uptime benchmarks, execute automated database backups every 24 hours, securely protect sensitive customer measurements, and ensure an intuitive user experience for non-technical staff evaluated through standard usability metrics. It is deployed in a cloud-based environment to provide high availability and real-time synchronization, allowing the system to handle multiple tailoring enterprises in Davao City efficiently.
In conclusion, this analysis ensures that Sutura is strategically aligned with the users' needs and the specific working environment of the local tailoring industry, providing a strong roadmap for the subsequent design and technical implementation of the system.

Lastly, through Manage Registration, the administrator reviews, approves, or rejects incoming shop applications. Together, these use cases establish the administrator as the central authority responsible for maintaining platform governance, managing system plans, and validating all tenant registrations. Technical Background
	•	Technologies to be Used
The development of SUTURA: A Web-Based Tailoring Shop Tracker System, will utilize modern, accessible, and industry-relevant technologies aligned with current standards in web application development. The system will adopt a client-server architecture, enabling centralized data management and multi-user access for tailoring shop owners, workshop staff, and clients through a single unified platform. The system will be fully responsive, ensuring optimal usability across desktop computers, tablets, and mobile browsers without requiring a separate mobile application.
The system will utilize Next.js, React, and TypeScript to build a dynamic and strictly-typed frontend. This stack, paired with Tailwind CSS, ensures a professional and fully responsive interface that adapts seamlessly to any screen size. To manage complex data flows and ensure a smooth user experience, Zustand will be utilized for efficient, lightweight state management across all system modules.
On the backend, the system will utilize Laravel (PHP) to manage all server-side operations, including business logic, user authentication, role-based access control, and data processing. Laravel will expose RESTful APIs to facilitate seamless communication between the frontend interface and the server, ensuring efficient and real-time data exchange across all core system modules such as order progress tracking, measurement repositories, and subscription processing workflows.
For data storage, MySQL will serve as the primary relational database management system, providing a structured and reliable schema for managing customer profiles, body measurements, job orders, billing records, appointment schedules, and transaction history. MySQL is well-suited for Sutura's operations as it supports structured relational data, which is essential for securely linking specific customer profiles to their unique physical dimensions and sequential order tracking milestones.
The database will be hosted on PlanetScale, a scalable cloud-based MySQL hosting platform, ensuring data availability, security, and remote accessibility without the need for on-premise server infrastructure.
For deployment, the frontend application will be hosted using Vercel, a cloud hosting platform optimized for modern web applications, while the Laravel backend and its API services will be deployed on Railway or Render. These platforms ensure that the system remains accessible online throughout the development and testing phases of the project.
For the development environment, the team will use Visual Studio Code as the primary code editor due to its extensive support for PHP, JavaScript, and the frameworks used in this project.
GitHub will be utilized for version control and collaborative development, allowing team members to manage, track, and merge code changes efficiently throughout the development lifecycle.
Postman will be used to test and validate all RESTful API endpoints, ensuring system reliability, data integrity, and consistent performance before integration.
Additionally, Figma will be used for designing user interface wireframes and interactive prototypes prior to development, serving as the visual and structural blueprint for all system screens, user flows, and design components.

Design of Software, System, Product, and/or Processes
	•	Database Design
The database design of SUTURA is engineered to provide a structured, secure, and scalable data storage architecture for the multi-tenant tailoring business management system. Utilizing a relational database model, system data is organized into interconnected tables that streamline user registration, active subscription cycles, storefront discovery, precise measurement versioning, job order tracking, and interactive analytics. (Refer to Page (93) for Figure (43)) 

The Admin and User Schema establishes the foundational identity management, role-based boundaries, and operational accountability of the platform. The USER table acts as the central authentication repository, storing credentials, profile metadata, system roles, and account verification statuses. Administrative operations, system state changes, and critical modifications are captured chronologically inside the audit_log table. This ensures complete transparency, strict security monitoring, and historical accountability across all tenant layers.

The Shop and Profile Schema structures storefront identities and their core service configurations. The SHOP table stores tenant business information including shop names, physical addresses, map coordinates, and admin verification states linking individual storefronts back to their respective owners in the user table. tailoring_staff_profile tracks localized employee metadata, shop associations, and working statuses. customer_profile holds specific contact parameters for end-users. Service maps out the merchant service catalogs, itemized pricing, and garment varieties displayed on the public marketplace.

The Subscription Schema controls multi-tenant billing models and system access permissions. The subscription_plan table defines the platform's standardized feature packages, itemizing plan names, billing cycles, and pricing tiers. Commercial relationships with active merchants are maintained inside the shop_subscription table, which records live plan selections, precise start and end dates, and tenant access states. This configuration maps directly to the financial tracking fields in the payment table to manage automated tier processing, plan renewals, and systemic feature restrictions.
The Order Tracking and Measurement Schema coordinates the production pipeline, anatomical configurations, and quality control workflows. Tailoring requests are captured in the job_order table, which coordinates customer ties, branch assignments, due dates, total costs, and production statuses. Underlying fabrication materials are tracked dynamically via the order_material table. To ensure measurement accuracy, the measuremnt table version-controls anatomical dimensions using structured JSON arrays linked to a client profile. Post-fulfillment loops terminate with customer payment settlements logged in the payment table and performance metrics captured in the feedback repository.

The Appointment Schema manages front-facing scheduling loops and client coordination mechanics. The appointment table logs booking timelines between customers and tailoring shops, tracking specific appointment dates, timeslots, target purposes (such as initial consultations or mid-production fitting sessions), and operational statuses. This framework prevents schedule conflicts, balances shop floor workloads, and streamlines automated client notifications across the marketplace network.
	
In summary, the normalized entity-relationship design balances database integrity with operational efficiency, establishing a high-performance backend architecture that safely handles concurrent transactions across the entire SUTURA ecosystem. Detailed attribute rules and data types for every table are provided in the data dictionary tables below. (Refer to Page (95) for Tables) 








Entity Relationship Diagram
The Sutura Entity-Relationship Diagram (ERD) defines the platform's multi-tenant data architecture and the relational connections between its core modules. It maps how system administrators, independent shop owners, client profiles, and workshop staff securely interact through normalized database tables and key constraints. By enforcing strict relational boundaries across security logs, monetization tiers, body measurements, job orders, and real-time scheduling data, the schema ensures absolute data integrity and multi-tenant isolation. 
The Administrative Module of the Entity-Relationship Diagram focuses on system security and accountability through a one-to-many relationship between the USER and audit_log entities. Each user account, uniquely identified by user_id and defined by access parameters like username, password, email, role, and status, acts as the authenticated actor executing modifications. These actions are systematically captured in the audit_log table, which tracks each event via log_id and links back to the user through the foreign key user_id. By logging the action_type, description, and a precise timestamp, this configuration ensures a transparent trail of operations for absolute security and administrative oversight. (Refer to Page (87) for Figure 35)) 
	The Subscription and Account Management Module of the Entity-Relationship Diagram focuses on platform monetization and shop tracking through a one-to-many relationship structure among three core tables. The subscription_plan entity stores tier definitions such as plan_id, plan_name, billing_cycle, and price. It connects to the shop_subscription ledger, which monitors enrollment metrics via subscription_id, start_date, end_date, and active status. Finally, the shop entity which holds storefront attributes like shop_id, owner_id, shop_name, address, coordinates, and verification_status links directly back to the subscription records. This configuration creates a relational bridge, ensuring that each registered shop is accurately tracked against its specific plan tier and billing cycle. (Refer to Page (88) for Figure (36)) 	
	

The Order Tracking and Measurement Module of the Entity-Relationship Diagram outlines the core data structures governing custom garment fabrication and payment tracking. The schema is centered around the job_order entity, which acts as the operational hub by linking foreign keys for customer_id, shop_id, service_id, staff_id, and measurement_id to manage real-time tracking metrics like status, total_cost, and due_date. It establishes one-to-many relationships across the module, connecting to the shop, staff_profile, customer_profile, measurement, and service tables to consolidate business operations, custom sizing data, and catalog choices. Finally, each order handles transaction accounting through the payment table, tracks fabric consumption via order_material, and logs customer ratings in the feedback ledger. This structured configuration ensures relational data integrity and provides a seamless lookup trail from initial body measurements to final order fulfillment. (Refer to Page (89) for Figure (37)) 
The Appointment Module of the Entity-Relationship Diagram outlines the data structures managing client bookings through a centralized scheduling framework. The schema is anchored by the appointment entity, which serves as the operational hub by linking the transaction metadata via foreign keys to both the shop and customer_profile tables. It establishes a one-to-many relationship structure across the module, where an individual shop can host multiple scheduled bookings, and a single customer can book multiple sessions over time. The appointment table records critical meeting details through attributes like appointment_id, schedule_date, purpose, and active reservation status. This configuration ensures strict relational data integrity, allowing the system to prevent calendar conflicts while keeping client scheduling tightly linked back to specific storefront operations. (Refer to Page (90) for Figure (38))

	•	Architectural
The structural model of SUTURA is defined through a series of class diagrams that outline the essential software entities, attributes, methods, and their logical relationships. By establishing these major system objects and their concrete operations, the diagrams provide the foundational blueprint for the platform’s object-oriented application logic and database architecture, ensuring a highly integrated, secure, and cohesive management ecosystem.
The Sutura system adopts a modular client-server architecture, utilizing RESTful APIs to facilitate seamless communication between the frontend and backend. This layered approach enables independent development, simplified maintenance, and high scalability across different platforms. To ensure security, the architecture implements Role-Based Access Control (RBAC), which strictly governs system access by assigning specific permissions to administrators, shop owners, tailoring staff, and customers according to their designated roles. 	

	The Sutura architecture utilizes the Model-View-Controller (MVC) pattern via the Laravel framework to clearly separate the user interface, business logic, and data handling. This is complemented by the Observer pattern, which manages system-triggered events such as appointment confirmations, payment updates, and automated notifications. By implementing these design patterns, the system ensures real-time responsiveness and reduced component coupling, providing a flexible and reliable foundation that supports long-term scalability. 

	The Administrative Module class diagram defines SUTURA's security, session management, and auditing structure through a direct one-to-many relationship between two core classes. The User class handles authentication, storing properties like user_id, username, password, email, role, and status, while exposing operations such as login(), logout(), resetPassword(), and getRole(). The AuditLog class records platform modifications by tracking a log_id, user_id, action_type, description, and timestamp. Through the recordAction() method, a single user can generate multiple log entries over time, providing a completely safe and verifiable audit trail for multi-tenant identity monitoring.  (Refer to Page (90) for Figure (39)) 
	The Subscription and Account Management class diagram defines how tenant shops acquire and maintain system access privileges through structured billing packages. The SubscriptionPlan class serves as the pricing template, defining properties like plan_id, plan_name, billing_cycle, and price along with methods like getPlanDetails() and updatePrice(). Through a one-to-many relationship, a single plan can define multiple active ShopSubscription records, which track membership cycles using attributes like subscription_id, start_date, end_date, and status, while exposing operations like activeSubscription(), checkValidity(), and renewSubscription(). Finally, each subscription record links directly via one-to-one relationship to a specific Shop profile, which contains core storefront details like shop_id, shop_name, address, and verification_status alongside its operational methods updateProfile(), toggleVisibility(), and getShopDetails().  (Refer to Page (91) for Figure (40)) 
	The Order Tracking and Measurement Module class diagram structures the core production pipeline, fabrication lifecycles, and feedback loops of a storefront. The architecture centers on the JobOrder class, which acts as the operational hub tracking properties like order_id, status, total_cost, and due_date through state management methods like calculateEstimatedPickup(), updateTrackingStatus(), and assignToTailor(). This controller maintains close structural dependencies with the Shop profile, the TailoringStaffProfile (tracking employee working_status), and the CustomerProfile. It links explicitly to the Service class to define pricing parameters, the OrderMaterial class to log resource consumption via logMaterialUsage(), and the JSON-driven Measurement class to save and retrieve physical dimensions using saveMeasurementData(). The workflow completes through a direct association with the Payment class to process financial transactions via processTransaction() and terminates at the Feedback class to register qualitative data points via submitReview(), providing seamless tracking from order placement to final pickup.  (Refer to Page (92) for Figure (41))

The Shop class serves as the core storefront entity representing the business, storing attributes like shop_id, owner_id, shop_name, address, coordinates, and verification_status while exposing methods like updateProfile(), toggleVisibility(), and getShopDetails(). Through a one-to-many relationship, each shop hosts active bookings managed by the Appointment class, which tracks reservation metadata including appointment_id, customer_id, shop_id, schedule_date, purpose, and status via programmatic methods like confirmAppointment(), cancelAppointment(), and reschedule(). Finally, the scheduling loop completes via a direct relationship where the CustomerProfile class containing core contact details like customer_id and phone_number triggers the booking process through operational interfaces like searchShops(), bookAppointment(), and getContactInfo(), providing a clear blueprint for organized client interactions. SUTURA Storyboard

PART 1 - ENTRY & ACCESS
Panel
Visual Description
User Action and System Response
1. SUTURA Landing Page
Premium landing page with SUTURA branding, short introduction, subscription teaser, and entry options: Browse as Guest, Login as Customer, Login as Shop Owner, and Login as Admin.
User selects how to enter the platform. The system routes the user either to the marketplace preview or the correct login page.
2. Login and Authentication
Login page with separate role access for Customer, Shop Owner, and System Admin.
User enters credentials. The system validates the account and redirects the user to the correct portal dashboard.

PART 2 — CUSTOMER MODULE
Panel
Visual Description
User Action and System Response
3. Marketplace Discovery
Marketplace interface with search bar, garment filters, tailoring shop cards, ratings, specialization tags, and Davao City map integration.
Customer searches garment types, filters tailoring shops by location and specialization, and browses tailoring shop results. The system displays matching tailoring shops dynamically.
4. Shop Profile and Consultation
Tailoring shop profile page containing services, gallery, pricing, ratings, branches, and consultation options.
Customer views tailoring shop details, requests consultation, books appointments, or saves the tailoring shop. The system stores the inquiry and appointment request.
5. Map-Based Interface
Interactive Davao City map containing red customer location pin and black tailoring shop pins.
Customers explore tailoring shops by area and open directions or branch information. The system updates visible shop pins based on selected filters.
6. Customer Orders and Measurements
Customer dashboard showing active tailoring orders, progress tracker, saved measurements, notifications, and saved tailoring shops.
Customers track garment progress, views measurement history, and checks order notifications. The system updates tailoring order progress and customer measurement records.
PART 3 - SHOP OWNER MODULE
Panel
Visual Description
User Action and System Response
7.Shop Owner Dashboard
Tailoring operations dashboard with KPI cards, order queue, appointments, measurements, service gallery, branch management, and sales reports.
Shop owner manages tailoring services, updates pricing, schedules appointments, records measurements, and monitors tailoring shop performance. The system updates operational shop data and customer transactions.
8.My Measurements Management
Advanced measurement management page containing customer profiles, reusable body measurements, garment sizing versions, fitting history, and tailoring preferences.
Shop owner or tailoring staff adds, edits, deletes, and updates customer measurement records and fitting information. The system stores reusable tailoring measurements and garment versions.



PART 4 - SYSTEM ADMIN MODULE
Panel
Visual Description
User Action and System Response
9. Administrative Dashboard
Administrative command center with tailoring shop approvals, subscription monitoring, account management, reports, and platform activity overview.
System administrator reviews tailoring shop registrations, validates apparel categories and branch locations, manages subscriptions, and oversees platform-wide activity. The system updates approval and subscription records.
PART 5 - ANALYTICS & REPORTING MODULE
Panel
Visual Description
User Action and System Response
10. Analytics and Reports
Interactive analytics dashboard with KPI summaries, subscription activity, monthly sales revenue, outstanding balances, and tailoring shop productivity charts.
Shop owners and system administrators review reports and analytics. The system generates real-time business insights and visual performance reports for decision-making.
